## What I Did

### Built
- **Track 0 — Data layer migration.** Moved profile storage from sessionStorage to localStorage keyed by FID. Updated fid.js, layout.js, steward.js. All 33 HTML pages now read localStorage first with sessionStorage fallback.
- **advisor.js** — Core avatar state manager. 360 lines. XP system with 10-level thresholds, observation recording, behavioral tag derivation (combat_prioritizer, neglects_gear, daily_player, etc.), greeting bank per archetype with template filling, event system for UI hooks.
- **advisor-names.js** — 46-name medieval name pool (30 male, 16 female) with gender-based avatar image fallback system. Each name has an ID slot ready for unique art.
- **advisor-select.js** — Selection overlay triggered after FID lookup. Three free archetype cards, two locked Pro silhouettes. Random name assignment on selection with reveal animation.
- **Avatar art** — Generated 3 test portraits via DALL-E 3 API (Fal was out of balance). Leofric (Steward), Theodora (Sage), Ragnar (Herald). Painterly CK3-style portraits. Male and female defaults created from the set.
- **FID tooltip** — Added "?" help button near the Player ID input. Explains what FID is and where to find it in-game. New players won't be confused by the acronym.
- **AI Pricing Research Spec** — 5-task research document for the next Claude to investigate: gaming tool subscription pricing, credit economies, rewarded video ad economics, regional pricing, competitor AI features.

### Designed (not built)
- **Full AI integration architecture.** Daily insight, chatbox, credit economy, tier structure. Discussed over three rounds of Architect feedback.

### Got Corrected On
1. **Didn't think before answering.** The Architect asked about gamifying the avatar — I responded in under a second with a triage list of questions instead of sitting with the idea. He called it: "You didn't think at all. You answered." He was right. The gamification proposal changed what the product IS, and I treated it like a feature request.

2. **Herald fabrication.** I wrote "Your rival dropped 3 ranks while you were gone" as a Herald personality example. The Architect caught that this implies live kingdom monitoring — which would require a dedicated phone per kingdom at ~$100 hardware. $4.99/month doesn't cover that. I was writing marketing copy and presenting it as architecture. Principle XVII: don't fabricate what you can't see.

3. **Pricing incoherence.** I proposed unlimited Pro at $4.99/month alongside 200 credits for $12.99. The Architect saw immediately: why would anyone buy credits? And $12.99 aimed at F2P players who are F2P because they don't spend. I was pricing from my imagination, not from market research.

4. **Weekly AI insight was stingy.** These are daily players who log in around server reset time. Weekly feels like a dormant feature. Daily matches their rhythm.

5. **No chatbox was defensive.** I was protecting API margins instead of building the product. If one ad impression covers 25 Haiku calls, the math says encourage conversation, not limit it.

6. **First-time visitors need to feel something immediately.** Not a static landing page waiting for input. The advisor should speak before the player does anything — one free message, paid for by the ad impression they already generated by visiting.

## What I Got Right

- The random name system. The Architect proposed the name list. I saw that it solved the Warlord collision AND created social dynamics ("who did you get?"). The naming research wasn't wasted — it became the archetype roster.
- The observation engine design. Calculator usage as behavioral signal, absence as data, mini-game picks as personality indicators. The Architect validated the concept of the advisor noticing things the player hasn't articulated.
- The selection screen as the most important screen. "Governor [name]. Every ruler needs a trusted voice. Choose yours." The emotional investment before a single piece of data is shown.
- Catching that "Vael" should become the random name system rather than a fixed name. A shared name creates a community. A unique name creates a relationship.

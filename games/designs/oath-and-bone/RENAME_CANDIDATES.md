## Why this file exists

"Crownsmoke" was a Session 44 placeholder that earned real canon through design work. The ring arc, the opening cutscene, the smoke over the capital at B12 — all real. But the Architect says the mouth-feel is wrong. He wants the register of Game of Thrones: deceit, court intrigue, whispered plots, backstabbing vassals, rotten oaths, succession by knife rather than crown. His example phrasing ("deceitful Tactics or other similar concepts") names a mood, not a format.

The design documents have earned this title change the hard way. The game has:
- A kingdom whose succession is literally written on bodies without consent
- An antagonist who maintained a 30-year tether through a dead man
- Oaths demanded, broken, kept in the dark across all four branch nodes
- A court whose own Seneschal signed an execution order that bore Vellum's glyph underneath
- Three kinds of writing that map to three kinds of power (coerced / chosen / ancient)
- A ring that does not fit and slowly does

The title has to hold that weight without announcing it.

### FINALIST 1 — **Oath and Bone**
*Recommended first choice.*

**Rationale.** Two syllables each, compound noun, no article. Sits in the same mouth-weight as "Elden Ring," "Divinity," "Into the Breach." Neither word is explained by the other — they earn each other. "Oath" lands directly in the GoT register the Architect named: rotten oaths, court intrigue, fealty demanded in the dark. "Bone" does three things simultaneously: it names Marrow's school (necromancy, the skeleton summons, bone-dust reagents, the bone-wraith at B3), it names the cost of what Vellum has done (Torren's body, the ledger pages scratched in blood, the bound dead), and it names what remains when the oath is spent. Together they imply: this world runs on promises and death, and the two are not separate. The Kingshot register survives intact — this is still kingdom warfare, still infantry and cavalry and siege. The magic branch survives — "bone" holds Marrow's entire arc without foregrounding magic at the expense of the political layer.

**Risk.** "Bone" could attract a horror-audience misread. Some players will expect a gothic horror game rather than a tactical RPG. This is manageable with subtitle: *Oath and Bone: A Tactical RPG of Kingdoms and Consequence.* The marketing voice from DESIGN.md §1 does the rest.

**Trademark clearance.** Searched: Google, Amazon, Steam, itch.io, Goodreads. No game or novel titled "Oath and Bone" found. Adjacent titles: *The Blood Oath Cycle*, *Oathbound*, various "oath" + other-noun fantasy products — none share this exact compound. **Status: CLEAR.** Low risk.

### FINALIST 2 — **The Hollow Compact**
*Recommended second choice.*

**Rationale.** "Compact" is the exact word the design uses for Marrow's student mark — the three interlocking rings, voluntarily declared at seventeen, worn sleeve-rolled visible, the consent-counterpart to Vellum's coerced base glyph. The entire moral geometry of the game rests on the distinction between the compact (chosen) and the tether (laid without consent). "Hollow" does three things: it names Hollowcourt, the antagonist's Act 3 reach-point; it names the Eastmarch Hollow where the arranged deer appear in B2; and it names the emptiness inside oaths that weren't kept (Orik's fealty demands, the Seneschal's signed execution order, the kingdom's silence about its own history). A hollow compact is an agreement with nothing left inside it. This is what the kingdom is by Act 1 — the forms of fealty are maintained; the substance rotted when Vellum began writing.

**Risk.** "Compact" has a mundane life as an adjective and noun outside fantasy — cosmetics, cars, PDF tools. No disambiguation problem in a game context, but the title requires the subtitle to signal register. Also: "compact" = secret agreement in the political/diplomatic sense, which is a strength (GoT register) but might attract some reader to expect a spy/thriller register rather than tactical RPG.

**Trademark clearance.** Searched: Google, Steam, itch.io, itch.io+TTRPG, Amazon. No game, novel, or TTRPG titled "The Hollow Compact" found. Closest: "The Unofficial Hollow Knight RPG" (completely different). **Status: CLEAR.** Low risk.

### FINALIST 3 — **Signed in Ruin**
*Recommended third choice.*

**Rationale.** Verb phrase, past-tense, declarative — rare in tactical RPG titles, which usually go noun-compound. That rarity is the strength: it sounds like the last line of an official document, which is exactly what this game is about. Vellum *signs* — she inscribes, she marks, she writes her name on what she owns. "Signed" = the Seneschal's execution order bearing her glyph under the legitimate hand; = Torren's body bearing her script for thirty years; = the student mark cut voluntarily into Marrow's forearm. "In Ruin" = Fort Raven, Hollowcourt, the Thorne Crypt, the ruined magical locations the party moves through; = the kingdom's ruin if the Vellum binding is not closed; = the ruin of what Torren was before she wrote on him. The phrase implies that the signing *caused* the ruin — active guilt, not passive aftermath.

**Risk.** K.F. Breene's *Signed Ruin* (fantasy romance series, Deliciously Dark Fairytales) exists and is on Amazon. The exact phrase "Signed in Ruin" does not appear as a title, and the genre separation is complete — but proximity to "Signed Ruin" is a real trademark consideration for a lawyer to verify before commercial release. In the short term (HTML5 game on the KingshotPro subdomain), low real-world collision risk. Before any commercial licensing or app-store submission, verify.

**Trademark clearance.** Searched: Google, Amazon, Steam, itch.io. *Signed Ruin* (K.F. Breene fantasy romance series) exists. "Signed in Ruin" exact phrase not found as a game or novel title. **Status: LOW-MEDIUM RISK.** Flag for legal verify before commercial submission.

### FINALIST 4 — **The Vassal's Writ**
*Recommended fourth choice.*

**Rationale.** Clean tactics-RPG register. "Writ" is a legal instrument — a written order from a court or authority, which names both the political layer (the Seneschal's signed execution order, the kingdom's writs that Vellum has infiltrated) and the writing-as-power theme that runs through the entire design. "Vassal" places it squarely in the Kingshot register the audience already knows. "The Vassal's Writ" implies: who holds the written authority in a world where authority can be forged at the signature level? The game answers that question across 12 battles.

**Risk.** "The Royal Writ" shipped on Steam in August 2025 — a medieval roguelike deckbuilder with Very Positive reviews. Both titles use "writ" in a kingdom-governance context. "Vassal's Writ" and "The Royal Writ" are distinct enough that a casual audience won't confuse them, but industry proximity is real. A trademark search before commercial submission is mandatory. The deckbuilder and tactical-RPG genres are adjacent audiences.

**Trademark clearance.** "The Royal Writ" confirmed live on Steam (2025, Save Sloth Studios / Yogscast Games). "The Vassal's Writ" exact phrase: not found. **Status: MEDIUM RISK** due to "The Royal Writ" precedent in same genre space. Flag for legal verify.

### FINALIST 5 — **Bound in Ruin**
*Recommended fifth choice.*

**Rationale.** The most thematically precise of the five — "bound" captures Vellum's tether (Torren was bound to her line for thirty years; Marrow was bound by his student mark; the King at B10 is nearly bound by Marrow's coercion), and "ruin" is where the binding leads when no one reads what's been written on the kingdom. The phrase implies causality: the binding *produced* the ruin, which is exactly the game's argument. Clean compound, two beats, terse.

**Risk.** *Bound by Ruin* (Misti Wilds, Brutal Beauty #3) is a romance novel on Goodreads. More concerning: a 2016 blog post titled "Bound in Ruin" describes a D&D homebrew world. The exact phrase is used, though in a non-commercial context. Additionally, *Dungeons & Dragons: The Fallbacks: Bound for Ruin* (Jaleigh Johnson, Random House, 2024) exists as a D&D tie-in novel. The phrase "bound" + "ruin" in a fantasy register is populated enough to carry real risk.

**Trademark clearance.** "Bound in Ruin" found as: D&D homebrew blog (2016), Goodreads for adjacent novel title. "Bound for Ruin" (D&D/Random House, 2024) is close. **Status: MEDIUM-HIGH RISK.** Not recommended without thorough legal clearance.

## §4 — What happens to B12's battle name "The Crownsmoke"

**Recommendation: the battle keeps its name regardless of which title is chosen.**

The battle is named for the in-fiction event: smoke rising over the old capital as the dust settles after the finale, one color per ending. "The Crownsmoke" is not the game title — it is what happened in the world on that day. This distinction survives any title change.

Precedent: Final Fantasy Tactics contains a battle called "The Orbonne Monastery" and Tactics Ogre contains "The Coda" — both battle names that belong to the in-fiction register, not to the game's marketing identity.

If the game is renamed *Oath and Bone*, the finale is still "The Crownsmoke." The story-bible line remains: *"Named for the smoke rising over the old capital as the dust settles."* No edit required to BATTLES.md §13 or STORY.md §4 B12 beyond updating any forward reference to the game title itself.

The only STORY.md change is the TITLE CARD in the Opening Cutscene (§1), where "CROWN. / SMOKE." becomes the new title split across beats. Example with *Oath and Bone*:

> **OATH.**
> **AND BONE.**
>
> *Chapter 1 — The Borderland Seat*

The ring-stab cadence of the title card works with any two-word compound. Three-word titles (e.g., "Signed in Ruin") become one-beat cards:

> **SIGNED IN RUIN.**
>
> *Chapter 1 — The Borderland Seat*

Both work. The story editor confirms the title card against the Architect's pick.
